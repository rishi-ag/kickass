About this project

What:

  • WeRecycle was born from a thesis project that, concerned with waste increases and low recycling rates, found that technology and simple feedback could help increase recycling activity by 50%.
  • WeRecycle also has a tool that could address the main issue reported from 9 out 10 sustainability offices: Recycling accountability.
  • WeRecycle is a platform with an interactive bin top offered here with a $150 pledge, as well as the tracking software that allows access to uniquely focused data offered with a $250 or $350 pledge.
  • WeRecycle has currently tested its first and second generation handmade bin tops and is ready to start production at another level.
  • Just $10 gets your school/business/work nominated to get a bin here!

Who (founders):

Jenna Jambeck PhD in Env. Engineering, Co-developer of Marine Debris Tracker App, Member of eXXpedition Atlantic'14

Kyle Johnsen PhD in Computer Engineering, Co-developer of Marine Debris Tracker App

Eliana Mozo-Reyes MS in Environmental Engineering

Details...

If you back at the full rate of $150, you get a WeRecycle lid:

  • The lid is 20-inch by 10-inch by (fits common recycle bin bottoms). Color will be determined by the material source, we will offer you choices, when available.
  • The lid can be for a recycle bin that accepts containers only or for containers and paper (single stream).
  • The LED lights will be green. You can choose the default sound (quiet notification), customize a sound for your bin, or choose no sound.
  • You can also choose to have a sticker placed on the top of the lid that has a photo/scannable WeRecycle logo (with a QR code) that will take people quickly to a webpage that tells them exactly what can or cannot go into the bin. You can also just get the sticker and place it anywhere else you would like the information (on the bottom of the bin or signage near the bin).

The bin is extremely low-powered by design to provide the minimally necessary feedback with the least amount of energy (we want net environmental benefits!). It operates off of batteries (not supplied), so that it does not require a nearby wall socket or cords. The batteries should last several months and rechargable batteries may be used. With technology advances, we have a goal to use indoor solar power or other alternative energy sources in the future.

The WeRecycle bin shown here, is a second generation prototype that we have tested in various environments and are ready to take public on a larger scale. We previously produced the entire bin by hand, but this Kickstarter campaign will allow us to source the most environmentally conscious materials (e.g., recycled plastic) and responsible manufacturing and... our backers will be the first to recycle with a smile on a regular basis.

How does the bin work?

It is a simple concept and elegant design that provides positive impacts and results! The bin calls attention to it with LED lights that randomly blink. When someone places an item in the bin, the other lights come on making a "smile" and a counter increases by 1. Every time an item is added, the counter goes up. Kids, students, and adults love the immediate feedback from the counter ($150 version) Note:the counter also counts hands as can be seen in the video. We are working hard on on this new design where hands would not be counted, but we found that even if users stick their hands in the bin, we are still meeting, and even going beyond our intended goal of having people notice and use the bin, to having people enjoying interacting with the bin, even when they don't have to recycle. We also found it to be a bit of a recycle bin "water cooler" - people tend to gather around it to chat.

If you choose the wireless data submission option ($250 level), the bin will be the same, but you will also get a transmission device that will need to be plugged into a wall in wireless network proximity to the bin. The transmission device will use wireless networks to send data to the WeRecycle database, which will be displayed there, and available for display on your choice of websites as well.

If you choose the $350 bin, you will get one year of data reporting on use of the bin prepared by WeRecycle (this data can be provided to funding agencies, the public, administrators, etc. wherever recycling activity data is required or desired).

The bins work great in any public or private location. Students, kids and adults love them. The smile and counter provide simple feedback and motivation... and satisfaction when recycling, which can lead to positive change.

How can I get a bin?

You can purchase an entire bin top for $150 (without data transmission). You can also donate smaller amounts to the campaign and for $10 nominate an entity (municipality, school, business, etc.) to get a bin - make sure you know that the entity can use the bin and you will be responsible for accepting it as the nominator. Then anyone who specifies this entity when donating votes for it ($1 = 1 vote per each entity nominated). The entities with 150 votes each or the top entities (until we hit our limit of 250) will get bins, if the campaign is successful. If we exceed our goal, we will continue to give bins out to others on the nomination list as we are able to. Check our website frequently for standings - and don't forget to specify your nominee!

For example, to nominate your school and get it on a list to receive a bin (if it gets enough votes), just give $10 and specify the name of your school - after you pledge, you will receive a message through Kickstarter that asks you, where do you want your votes to go?. A daily list will be generated and posted to the WeRecycle site here. Then others can donate as little as $2 to the campaign (and mention your school) to help you get as many votes as possible to be in the top entities to get a bin (or if you reach 150 votes, you will get a bin).

People who contribute money but don't specify an entity for it to go to will have the money given to schools that still need donations at the end of the campaign.

Just like that - it won't take long for a few friends and family to help get bins into schools, municipalities or businesses!

Where do we envision these bins?

We envision WeRecycle bins in:

Classrooms/schools, for example - teachers could crowd-fund a bin to spread awareness and practice recycling in the classroom (containers might not be allowed in class, but paper could be recycled). With a data collection level bin ($250), they could have the bin send data to a computer immediately available to students. Later, the data can be used in lesson plans within the curriculum. e.g., When do people recycle most? Classrooms would be easy to monitor for hand counting and other interactions with the bin.

Universities, the WeRecycle lid fits many bin bottoms already in place at some higher education institutions. We don't recommend replacing all the lids with WeRecycle lids, just a select few. The goal is to, again, only use as many as needed to obtain the desired outcome (and increase in recycling).

Municipalities, e.g., library, courthouse, community building.

Coffee Shops and Cafes, Movie Theaters, Hospitals...

The list goes on!

 Can I have a bin at home?

Sure! This bin top would be fun to have at home. You could place it inside, in your garage or other fairly well-protected area (they are not meant for outdoor use) and your family members will always want to recycle! The bin would also be great for parties and events where people are often distracted and don't notice the recycling versus trash cans. It would certainly be a conversation piece.

How will the optional bin sticker work?

If you purchase a bin, you will have the option of getting a WeRecycle sticker with a QR code that can be scanned to let people know what can/can't be placed in the bin. This is, hands down, the most common question in people's mind when they approach a bin, "Can I recycle this [item] here?"

Who and what is WeRecycle?

WeRecycle was founded by three colleagues at the University of Georgia (UGA) College of Engineering: Dr. Jenna Jambeck, Dr. Kyle Johnsen and Eliana Mozo-Reyes. Eliana completed her masters research on the impact of an initial prototype of the bin on recycling at UGA. We found increases in both visits and quantity of recyclables in the WeRecycle bins versus regular bins at a football game in the UGA Student Center.

We strive to be a beneficial company

Our goal is social good. Jenna, Kyle and Eliana have been working on the idea of smartly using technology to promote sustainability, resiliency and restoration since 2010 - and WeRecycle was born out of this philosophy. The company will strive to be restorative, for example, our goal is for our product to produce a net environmental benefit through increasing capture of recyclables. We plan to manufacture domestically and responsibly, use lead-free solder and post-consumer recycled materials. We also have designed the WeRecycle bin with end-of-life in mind and will practice extended producer responsibility. We will take-back lids if people no longer want them or at their end-of-life for reuse (refurbishment), disassembly and/or recycling. And we encourage you to only purchase the minimal amount of lids to result in the intended positive impact.

Final thoughts...

Jenna has worked with the waste industry and research for over 17 years and knows that after waste reduction, our recycling rates need to increase, especially in public areas, and positive associations can create behavior changes. Kyle is a computer systems engineering whiz that can make ideas happen in real time. And Eliana, through her research, has witnessed the difference that small interaction and feedback from the WeRecycle bin can make in people's behavior.

We want to make recycling count. Be the first to recycle with a smile today! 

Risks and challenges

We have explored sourcing the manufacture of our circuit boards in the U.S., but delays could occur. The components have been built by hand for other prototypes and we are transitioning over to larger scale manufacturing, trying to keep the bin locally made and produced with recycled content and lead-free solder, for example. We would also like to take back lids at their end of life for reuse and disassembly and recycling. We will investigate this option if this campaign is successful. We are attempting to have the bin NOT count hands, but we might not be successful in this endeavor if we are limited in power. Our main goal is low power so that recycling increases are not "cancelled" by energy consumption of the bin. We want to produce an environmentally conscious product with a net benefit. If hands are counted, then reporting and contests would include these numbers, but the positive benefit of this is that people are interacting with a recycle bin and the positive impact to recycling (the main point) can still be realized. The team is well-qualified to address all these issues, but unforeseen delays or obstacles could mean the final product takes a bit longer or has limited capacity.

FAQ

  • What is inside the bin and how do you keep it extremely low powered to conserve energy?

    Inside the bin is a custom-built circuit designed to provide extremely low-power operation of the sensors, lights, and communication devices. To achieve months of expected battery life using only three C-cell batteries was a challenge. The batteries provide approximately 5000 milliamp-hours of available energy, or 5000 hours of use at 1mA of current. Powering a single LED at high brightness requires up to 20mA, which would yield about a month of battery life! For this reason, most of the time (approximately 99% of the time) the bin is resting, turning on for only a few thousandths of a second to check its sensors every 1/20th of a second and to very briefly blink the LEDs to draw attention to itself. When the sensors detect activity, the bin comes to life, showing counts, smiling, and communicating until eventually going back to its low energy state.
